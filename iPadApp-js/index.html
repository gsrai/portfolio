The Regent Street Cinema Campaign

The University of Westminster has launched a major campaign to restore one of the most important cinemas in Britain to its former glory, evoking its 19th century heritage, while looking to the future. As a major industry landmark and birthplace of British Cinema, the restored Regent Street Cinema and state-of-the-art auditorium will be open to the public as a unique arts venue for screenings, lectures, workshops and events, in the heart of London's West End. more...

Our heritage

Playing host to invention and wonder

The Polytechnic Institution was founded in 1838 and rapidly established itself as a London landmark known for demonstrating technical innovations of the day. Visitors could be submerged underwater in the newly developed diving bell or have their portrait taken in Europe's first photography studio. The Polytechnic became the University of Westminster in 1992 and today we boast a vibrant learning environment attracting more than 20,000 students from over 150 nations.

Name a Seat

Support the Regent Street Cinema in it's journey to transform back into a landmark venue for film and the future of film education.

To raise money to restore the Cinema, we are offering supporters the chance to name a seat and join 200 others in raising £200,000 towards securing the future of the Cinema.

You can name a seat for £750, or choose one of our exclusive Director's seats for £1500. Your gift will go directly towards the cost of restoration and will help secure the future of this historic venue.

To mark your support you will receive:

  • A personalised plaque attached to the seat bearing your chosen name for the lifetime of the seat.
  • Acknowledgement of your support on our website and in publications.
  • An invitation to view your seat.
  • One year membership of the Regent Street Cinema.

Balcony

The original balcony was removed in 1894 when Hogg re-developed the theatre into a more general 'hall', with gallery seating now running round all 4 sides of the room. This was, in turn, removed in 1926 to be replaced with a balcony in the art deco style.

Wall decoration

The majority of images of 1848 theatre of the Royal Polytechnic Institution are line drawings, and so give us little clue to the decoration. However two watercolours of 1882 show a richly decorated room with green walls and features picked out in red and gold. Black and white photographs of the auditorium around the turn-of-the-century appear to show that all these colours have gone in the 1894 refurbishment.

Floor

The floor level of the 1848 theatre was lower, with the seating descending from the entrance to the stage. Underneath the stage was a pit that was used to create the 'Pepper's Ghost' illusion.

Ceiling Light

When the cinema opened as a magic lantern theatre in 1848, it would have had no electric light. The primary means of lighting the theatre was from a skylight.

Organ Pipes

The pipes for the cinema organ are inside a wooden chamber at the back of the stage and this is where the sound is produced. When a key is pressed on the console, an electrical current carries the information to the pipes. This controls the flow of air through the pipes, and produces sound.

Projection room

The original 1848 projection or 'manipulating room' was at the back of the auditorium, between the two levels of seating. This was used for projecting Magic Lantern slides onto a large circular screen on the stage. The projecting room was removed during the 1894 refurbishment.

Proscenium

A proscenium arch is the frame around a theatre stage. The victorian Royal Polytechnic Institution theatre had a decorative and richly coloured proscenium arch, but this was removed in 1894, when the auditorium was converted into a meeting hall. However a new one was installed during the 1926 renovations, introducing the art deco styling that can still be seen today.

Iron Railings

In 1894 a gallery was installed around the auditorium. This replaced the original tiered seating of the 1848 theatre and turned it more into a meeting hall for lectures, debates, teaching and even boxing.

Seating

The 1848 theatre of the Royal Polytechnic Institution was purpose-built for magic lantern shows and held 1500 people, with shows often running twice a day. However the sales particular of 1881 described it as holding 'between 800 and 900 persons'. Quintin Hogg bought the building and converted it into a meeting hall, seating 1100 people, with an additional 150 on the platform. The seating numbers after the 1894 refurbishment - and therefore at the time of the Lumiere's Cinematographe screening - aren't confirmed but are believed to be roughly the same. We do know however that the audience were seated on loose wooden chairs, rather than the fixed plush seats of today.

Stage

The magic lantern slides used at the Royal Polytechnic Institution were larger than usual slides, at around 20cm x 15cm. The image was projected onto a circular oil canvas screen with a diameter of almost 9m, at a distance of 11m from the back of the theatre. This was achieved through the use of oxyhydrogen - or limelight as it was commonly known.

Organ Pipes

The pipes for the cinema organ are inside a wooden chamber at the back of the stage and this is where the sound is produced. When a key is pressed on the console, an electrical current carries the information to the pipes. This controls the flow of air through the pipes, and produces sound. The vibrato effect is created by manually shaking the air source. The sound produced by the organ is not amplified at all by electricity. Instead the volume is controlled by the flaps at the front of the chamber. As well as the pipes, there are real instruments inside the chamber such as a glockenspiel and sleigh bells.

Balcony

The original balcony was removed in 1894 when Hogg re-developed the theatre into a more general "hall", with gallery seating now running round all 4 sides of the room. This was, in turn, removed in 1926 to be replaced with a balcony in the art deco style.

Seating

The 1848 theatre of the Royal Polytechnic Institution was purpose-built for magic lantern shows and held 1500 people, with shows often running twice a day. Quintin Hogg bought the building and converted it into a meeting hall, seating 1100 people, with an additional 150 on the platform. The seating numbers after the 1894 refurbishment - and therefore at the time of the Lumiere's Cinematographe screening - aren't confirmed but are believed to be roughly the same. We do know however that the audience were seated on loose wooden chairs, rather than the fixed plush seats of today. As audiences came to expect comfort as much as spectacle, the number of seats in the cinema reduced. After the 1926 rebuilding, the cinema only seated 610. By comparison, the 1928 Empire Theatre in Leicester Square seated 3300. Further seats seem to have been removed with each successive refurbishment and by the time it closed as the Regent Theatre in 1979, the auditorium seated only 517.

Proscenium

A proscenium arch is the frame around a theatre stage. The victorian Royal Polytechnic Institution theatre had a decorative and richly coloured proscenium arch, but this was removed in 1894, when the auditorium was converted into a meeting hall. However a new one was installed during the 1926 renovations, introducing the art deco styling that can still be seen today.

Black and white photographs of the auditorium around the turn-of-the-century appear to show that all these colours have gone in the 1894 refurbishment.

Ceiling Light

When the cinema opened as a magic lantern theatre in 1848, it would have had no electric light. The primary means of lighting the theatre was from a skylight. This was covered by movable shutters during daytime performances and which enabled the theatre 'in an instant to restore the light again for the general promenade of the public'. The skylight was covered during the 1926 re-build as additional classrooms were added overhead. An electric light was therefore installed in the ceiling instead.

Floor

The floor level of the 1848 theatre was lower, with the seating descending from the entrance to the stage. Underneath the stage was a pit that was used to create the 'Pepper's Ghost' illusion. The floor was levelled when Quintin Hogg purchased the building, and then raised further during the 1894 re-build, reducing the height of the auditorium from 50ft to 30ft.

Stage

The magic lantern slides used at the Royal Polytechnic Institution were larger than usual slides, at around 20cm x 15cm. The image was projected onto a circular oil canvas screen with a diameter of almost 9m, at a distance of 11m from the back of the theatre. This was achieved through the use of oxyhydrogen - or limelight as it was commonly known. The special large format slides enabled a clear and detailed image to be seen on the screen, even at this size and distance.

Projection room

The original 1848 projection or 'manipulating room' was at the back of the auditorium, between the two levels of seating. This was used for projecting Magic Lantern slides onto a large circular screen on the stage. The projecting room was removed during the 1894 refurbishment. When the Lumiere's Cinematographe was exhibited in 1896, it was projected from the floor of the auditorium onto a temporary screen. The projection room above the balcony was installed during the 1926 rebuild. In order to achieve clear sight lines, part of the original iron girders in the ceiling had to be removed.

Iron Railings

In 1894 a gallery was installed around the auditorium. This replaced the original tiered seating of the 1848 theatre and turned it more into a meeting hall for lectures, debates, teaching and even boxing. By 1926 the majority of the gallery was removed, in favour of the balcony we see today. However, for reasons that are not clear, the portion behind the stage was left, giving us a glimpse into the past.
